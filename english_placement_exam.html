Passage A — Short Conversation
Listen to or read the dialogue, then answer the questions.
Dialogue at a Hotel
~45 seconds · Beginner – Intermediate
Receptionist: Good afternoon! Welcome to the Grand Hotel. How can I help you?
Guest: Hello! I have a reservation. My name is James Collins.
Receptionist: Let me check… Yes, a double room for three nights. Is that correct?
Guest: Yes, that's right. We also requested a room with a sea view, if possible.
Receptionist: Of course! I'll upgrade you to a room on the fourth floor. Breakfast is included from 7 to 10 every morning.
Guest: Wonderful, thank you so much!
PASSAGE A — Questions:
A1. What kind of room did the guest book?
A2. For how many nights is the reservation?
A3. What time does breakfast end?

Reading Comprehension

Read each text carefully and answer the questions — 2 passages

Text 1 — A Change of Pace
Intermediate level · Read the text, then answer the questions below.

After twenty-five years working as an accountant in a busy city office, Margaret decided it was time for a change. At the age of fifty-two, she left her job, sold her apartment, and moved to a small coastal village in southern Portugal. Her friends thought she had lost her mind.

"People kept asking me, 'But what will you do there?'" she laughs. "And I told them — I'll learn Portuguese, I'll grow vegetables, I'll read all the books I never had time for."

Two years later, Margaret runs a small bed-and-breakfast from her whitewashed cottage, where she hosts travelers from around the world. She wakes up at six every morning, not because she has to, but because she wants to watch the sun rise over the Atlantic.

"I'm not saying everyone should quit their job and move abroad," she says. "But I do think we spend too much time doing what we're supposed to do, and not enough time doing what makes us feel alive."

TEXT 1 — Questions:
R1. What did Margaret do for a living before her big change?
R2. How did her friends react to her decision?
R3. What does Margaret do now?
R4. What is the main message of Margaret's final quote?
R5. In your own words, why do you think Margaret wakes up at 6 AM now, even though she doesn't have to? Write 1–2 sentences.
Text 2 — The Science of Sleep
Upper Intermediate level · Read and answer the questions.

For decades, sleep was considered a passive state — a time when the brain simply switched off and rested. Modern neuroscience has completely overturned this idea. Far from being inactive, the sleeping brain is extraordinarily busy, performing essential tasks that waking life simply cannot accomplish.

One of sleep's most critical functions is memory consolidation. While we sleep, the hippocampus — the brain's short-term memory center — transfers information to the cerebral cortex for long-term storage. This is why students who sleep well after studying consistently outperform those who stay up all night reviewing their notes.

Sleep also plays a vital role in physical health. During deep sleep, the body releases growth hormone, repairs damaged tissue, and strengthens the immune system. Chronic sleep deprivation, on the other hand, has been linked to an increased risk of obesity, heart disease, and even certain types of cancer.

Despite this evidence, many adults in modern societies treat sleep as a luxury rather than a necessity. The average adult now sleeps one to two hours less per night than people did fifty years ago. Researchers warn that this widespread sleep loss represents a "silent public health crisis" that deserves far more attention than it currently receives.

TEXT 2 — Questions:
R6. According to the text, what was the old understanding of sleep?
R7. What happens in the hippocampus during sleep?
R8. Which of the following is NOT mentioned as a risk of chronic sleep deprivation?
R9. What does the phrase "silent public health crisis" suggest?
R10. Do you think sleep is treated as a priority in your daily life? Why or why not? Write 3–4 sentences using evidence from the text.

Spoken English

Record your responses — speak naturally and at your own pace

This section is evaluated live with your tutor. Record your answers, or answer verbally during your session. There are no wrong responses — we are listening for fluency, confidence, and vocabulary range.
Oral 1 — Introduce yourself
Please talk about yourself for about 1–2 minutes. Include: your name, where you are from, what you do for work, and why you want to learn English.
💡 Tip: Don't worry about perfection. Just speak naturally!
Oral 2 — Daily Routine
Describe a typical day in your life. What time do you wake up? What do you do in the morning, afternoon, and evening? Use as many details as you can.
💡 Try to use time expressions: "first," "then," "after that," "finally."
Oral 3 — Opinion Question
Do you think technology has made our lives better or worse? Give your opinion and at least two reasons to support it.
💡 Use phrases like "In my opinion…", "I think that…", "For example…", "On the other hand…"
